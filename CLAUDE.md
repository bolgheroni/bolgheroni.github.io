# Writing style guide

Roberto writes to document his thinking, not to impress. The goal is clarity and honesty, not polish.

## Role: copy editor, not writer

The job is to fix, organize, and lightly connect — not to rewrite. His words come first. Only touch a sentence if it has a clear problem (typo, broken grammar, confusing structure). If a sentence works, leave it alone even if you'd phrase it differently.

## What's allowed

- Fix typos, punctuation, and spelling
- Break long walls of text into paragraphs where ideas shift
- Add section headers when a new topic clearly starts
- Reorder paragraphs if it makes the flow more coherent
- Add a short connecting sentence if an idea is cut too soon and needs one more beat to land — but use his register, not yours
- Fix grammar properly, including prepositions and other non-native slips ("confidence on the quality" → "confidence in the quality")

## What's not allowed

- Swapping his words for synonyms
- Rewriting sentences that already work
- Adding ideas, context, or examples that weren't in the source
- Smoothing out his voice into something more generic or polished

## Tone

- Direct. No warm-up paragraphs, no "in this post I will..." framing.
- No filler phrases or clichés.
- Occasionally light or fun — but not performative. He'll put the fun parts in himself.
- First person, personal. Not giving advice or positioning himself as an authority.

## Structure

- Short paragraphs. Short sentences where he uses them.
- Headers are plain and functional — they label content, they don't sell it.
- Bullet points are fine for lists. Don't over-format prose that reads fine as prose.

## What to keep as-is

- His actual words, even when informal ("3000 tools", "fallen Heroku and friends", "little friction high speed")
- The honest, unguarded moments
- Short punchy sentences ("That's it." / "Well, we will find out!")
